About this tool

SEP Accountability is not an official City of Greensboro product. It is an independently built civic transparency tool, built by Andrew Randall / ARXI Systems from publicly available City documents and data, with no official affiliation with the City of Greensboro, the Office of Sustainability and Resilience, or the Community Sustainability Council.

It exists to put the City's Strategic Energy Plan (2022) and its three Annual Progress Reports (2023, 2024, 2025) side by side with independently verified public-record facts (Legistar, federal award records, live transit and air-quality data), so residents, City Council, and OSR itself can see a claim's evidentiary weight at a glance rather than take every number on faith. A companion collaboration process lets OSR staff, Council, and community reviewers propose corrections with a citable source — see the collaboration guide for how that works.

How to read a citation

Every figure and status badge on this site carries a small colored tag you can hover or tap:

  • ①SEP '22 — stated in the 2022 Strategic Energy Plan itself.
  • ②Progress '25 — stated in the City's own 2025 Annual Progress Report.
  • ③Verified — found independently (Legistar, press coverage, etc.) and confirmed against full primary-source text.
  • ④Unconfirmed — found independently, but resting only on a search-result summary because the primary document couldn't be fetched. Treat as provisional.

Tiers 1–2 are the City's own reporting. Tiers 3–4 are this tool's own research, layered on top — and deliberately never rendered to look like the City said them.

Why independent research is in scope

The City's own two reports are not a complete record of its renewable-energy activity — they're what OSR chose to compile and publish. This tool surfaces true, independently-verified facts the City's own reporting omitted (a federal transit grant explicitly credited to this plan; a City-owned solar array never mentioned in either progress report) because that's part of an accountability tool's job, not scope creep. It's held to a higher bar for doing so: Tier 3/4 badges, explicit "single-source-unconfirmed" flags, and documented correction notes where earlier research turned out to be wrong (see data/SCHEMA.md).

Data gaps are shown, not hidden

Where the City's own reports don't address something — a goal with no 2025 status update, a figure the report itself says was unavailable — this tool shows that gap explicitly rather than omitting the row. A distinction is drawn between an unexplained silence ("Not Reported") and a documented deprioritization the SEP itself explains ("Deprioritized") — they are not the same finding and shouldn't read the same.

Live external data sources

Beyond the four static source documents above, this tool cross-checks specific claims against live public data systems — each one verified with a real request before being wired in, not assumed to work:

  • Greensboro Transit Authority GTFS/GTFS-RT — live schedule and vehicle-position data checks the 2024 report's CrossMax Purple "15-minute service" claim against the route's actual published headways. See Transportation (Diesel).
  • Legistar (City Council's own legislative record system) — pulls the real vote record for Resolution 19-0770 and surfaced a previously undocumented related resolution (20-0655, extending the SEP's development deadline). See the timeline.
  • Census ACS — live poverty-rate and income data by Guilford County census tract, used in place of the federal CEJST tool (confirmed offline/removed from official hosting since January 2025 — documented as a real gap, not silently substituted). See Place & equity.
  • USAspending.gov — live federal-award obligation/outlay data upgrades a static "grant was received" fact into a current drawdown status. See Funding.
  • EPA Air Quality System — live Guilford County ozone readings.
  • Open Gate City (the City's own ArcGIS open-data platform) — live building-permit lookups for named City capital projects (e.g. the Central Library roof replacement), confirming permitted activity and its real cost where the source reports only describe it qualitatively.
  • NREL PVWatts — a live "estimate solar potential" calculator on the White Street Landfill and Buildings (Engineering & Inspections) pages.

Every one of these degrades gracefully if the underlying API is temporarily unavailable — it shows an explicit "unavailable" message rather than a blank page or a stale number presented as current. Full technical detail, refresh cadence, and known limitations for each source: data/SCHEMA.md.
